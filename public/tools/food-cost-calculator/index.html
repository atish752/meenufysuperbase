What Is Food Cost Percentage? (And Why It Makes or Breaks Your Restaurant)

Food cost percentage is the single most important number in restaurant finance. It measures what proportion of your revenue disappears into ingredient costs — and it determines whether your restaurant is genuinely profitable or just "busy broke."

The formula is simple: divide your total food costs by your total food sales, then multiply by 100. If you spent ₹28,000 on ingredients this week and earned ₹90,000 in food sales, your food cost percentage is 31.1%.

The problem most restaurant owners face: they price their menus on instinct, not math. A dish priced at ₹250 might feel profitable — until you calculate that the ingredients cost ₹105, giving you a 42% food cost and virtually no margin after labor and rent.

This calculator eliminates that guesswork.

Two Ways to Calculate Food Cost Percentage

There are two distinct calculations every restaurant should run — and most owners only know one.

1. Per-Dish Food Cost Percentage (Recipe Costing)

This tells you the profitability of a single menu item. It's the foundation of intelligent menu pricing.

Food Cost Per Dish = Sum of all ingredient costs for one portion

Food Cost % = (Food Cost Per Dish ÷ Selling Price) × 100

Example:
Your Chicken Tikka Masala uses:
- Chicken (150g at ₹280/kg) = ₹42.00
- Cream (50ml at ₹80/L) = ₹4.00
- Tomatoes, spices, oil = ₹8.50
- Total ingredient cost: ₹54.50

If you sell it for ₹195:
Food Cost % = (54.50 ÷ 195) × 100 = 27.9% (Excellent)

To find the RIGHT selling price: divide your ingredient cost by your target food cost percentage.
₹54.50 ÷ 0.30 = ₹181.67 → Round up to ₹185 or ₹190.

2. Overall Food Cost Percentage (Period-Based COGS Method)

This tells you how efficiently your kitchen is using food across all dishes — including waste, over-portioning, and spoilage that recipe costing won't catch.

COGS = Opening Inventory + Purchases During Period − Closing Inventory

Food Cost % = (COGS ÷ Total Food Sales) × 100

Example (monthly):
- Opening inventory: ₹1,20,000
- Purchases: ₹85,000
- Closing inventory: ₹95,000
- COGS = 1,20,000 + 85,000 − 95,000 = ₹1,10,000
- Total food sales: ₹3,40,000
- Food Cost % = (1,10,000 ÷ 3,40,000) × 100 = 32.4% (Standard)

If your per-dish food cost % is 29% but your overall food cost % is 36%, that 7% gap is your waste, theft, and over-portioning problem — and it's costing you real money every week.

The universal rule: if your food cost percentage consistently exceeds 38%, you are likely losing profitability on the plate and need to act — either raise prices, reduce ingredient costs, control portions, or reduce waste.

7 Proven Ways to Reduce Your Restaurant's Food Cost

1. Calculate the Cost of Every Dish (Start Here)

Most restaurants that overspend on food have never actually costed their recipes properly. Use this calculator to find every dish on your menu, identify which ones have food costs above 35%, and either re-price them or re-engineer the recipe.

2. Implement Strict Portion Control

A single extra 30g of protein per dish, multiplied across 200 covers daily, is 6kg of protein over-served per day. At ₹280/kg, that's ₹1,680 vanishing daily — ₹50,400 per month. Standardize your portions with kitchen scales, portion cups, and ladle sizes.

3. Conduct Weekly Inventory Counts

The difference between your theoretical food cost (from recipes) and your actual food cost (from COGS) is your "variance" — and variance is profit leaking somewhere. Weekly counts keep waste, theft, and spoilage visible before they compound.

4. Use the "First In, First Out" (FIFO) Method

Store and use older inventory before newer stock. FIFO prevents spoilage by ensuring nothing gets buried in the walk-in and forgotten. Restaurants using FIFO consistently report 2–5% lower food waste costs.

5. Negotiate Supplier Contracts Based on Volume

Once you know your exact ingredient costs, you have data to negotiate. Consolidate purchases with fewer suppliers in exchange for better per-unit pricing. Even a 5% reduction in ingredient costs can swing a 28% food cost to 26.6% — a meaningful difference at scale.

6. Redesign Your Menu Around High-Margin Items

Menu engineering categorizes your dishes into Stars (high profit, high popularity), Plowhorses (popular but low margin), Puzzles (high margin, low popularity), and Dogs (low on both). Use this framework to spotlight your Stars, re-engineer your Plowhorses, and eliminate your Dogs.

7. Reduce Order-Taking Errors That Lead to Food Waste

Handwritten orders misread by kitchen staff result in dishes remade from scratch — doubling the ingredient cost of that order. Digital QR ordering systems eliminate order-entry errors entirely, since the customer's order goes directly to the kitchen display without a waiter transcribing it.

Common Food Cost Calculation Mistakes (And How to Avoid Them)

Mistake 1: Forgetting Opening Inventory

The most frequent error in overall food cost calculation. If you only count purchases, you understate your COGS, making food costs appear lower than they actually are. Always record the value of stock at the start of every period.

Mistake 2: Including Non-Food Items in COGS

Napkins, straws, cleaning supplies, and packaging inflate your food cost percentage if you include them. Track these separately as "consumables" or "supplies." Your food cost should only measure ingredients.

Mistake 3: Ignoring Wastage and Yield Loss

A recipe that calls for 200g of chicken breast doesn't account for the 20–25% of weight lost during trimming and cooking. If you don't apply a yield factor, your costed selling price will be too low. Use the wastage adjustment in this calculator to account for cooking loss.

Mistake 4: Calculating Only Once Per Quarter

Food costs change every week. Ingredient prices fluctuate with seasons, supply chains, and currency movements. A price that kept food costs at 30% in January might push you to 37% by March if you haven't recalculated. Build weekly recipe costing into your routine.

Mistake 5: Pricing From Competitors, Not From Costs

"The restaurant next door charges ₹250 for this, so I'll charge ₹240" is how restaurants quietly go broke. Competitive pricing is part of the picture, but if your costs are higher than your competitor's (perhaps they buy in greater volume), you cannot profitably match their price. Start with your food cost, set the minimum viable price, then look at competitors.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the ideal food cost percentage for a restaurant? ▼
Most successful restaurants target a food cost percentage between 28% and 35% of total food sales. Fine dining restaurants often achieve 25–30% due to premium pricing power. Fast food and QSR concepts typically run 25–32% thanks to volume and standardization. If your food cost consistently exceeds 38–40%, your menu is likely underpriced or your ingredient costs are too high.
How do I calculate food cost percentage? ▼
There are two methods. For a single dish: divide the total ingredient cost by the selling price, then multiply by 100. For your overall restaurant: use COGS (Beginning Inventory + Purchases − Ending Inventory) divided by Total Food Sales, multiplied by 100. This free calculator handles both calculations instantly.
How do I find the right selling price for a dish? ▼
Divide your total ingredient cost by your target food cost percentage. If a dish costs ₹90 to make and you target a 30% food cost, your minimum selling price is ₹90 ÷ 0.30 = ₹300. This calculator computes the recommended selling price automatically.
Does this food cost calculator include labor costs? ▼
No — this tool calculates ingredient food cost only, which is the most controllable variable in daily operations. Labor, rent, utilities, and overhead are separate cost categories. Many operators add 5–10% to their food cost target as a buffer to account for these additional costs.
What is the difference between food cost and COGS? ▼
Food cost typically refers to ingredient costs for a specific dish or menu item. COGS (Cost of Goods Sold) is a broader accounting term that covers all the ingredients consumed during a period — including waste, spoilage, and staff meals. Your overall food cost percentage is calculated from COGS.
Why is my actual food cost higher than my theoretical food cost? ▼
The difference (called "variance") is caused by waste, over-portioning, theft, spoilage, or unrecorded staff consumption. If your recipes suggest a 29% food cost but your COGS-based calculation shows 36%, that 7% gap represents money leaking from your kitchen. Weekly inventory counts help identify and plug these leaks.
How often should I calculate my food cost percentage? ▼
Per-dish food costs should be reviewed whenever ingredient prices change (at minimum quarterly). Your overall food cost percentage should be calculated weekly for active management — monthly at absolute minimum. Restaurants that track this weekly consistently outperform those that check it quarterly.
Can I use this calculator for a café or coffee shop? ▼
Yes. This calculator works for any food or beverage business — restaurants, cafés, coffee shops, food trucks, cloud kitchens, catering businesses, bakeries, and bars. Select your local currency and add your ingredients. Cafés typically target a blended food and beverage cost of 28–40%.
What is a good food cost percentage for a food truck? ▼
Food trucks typically target 28–40% food cost, with more tolerance for higher percentages because they have no rent and lower overhead. However, targeting 30–35% is ideal for maximum profitability, especially as ingredient costs fluctuate.
How do I reduce my restaurant's food cost percentage? ▼
The seven most effective methods are: (1) Cost every recipe using this calculator, (2) implement strict portion control with scales, (3) conduct weekly inventory counts, (4) use FIFO stock rotation, (5) negotiate with suppliers using volume, (6) remove low-margin items from your menu, and (7) eliminate order errors with digital ordering systems.
